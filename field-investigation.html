Field Investigation & On-Site Research

Some answers can't be found in archives or databases. They're buried in remote locations, forgotten in private estates, or hidden in plain sight. Field investigation is where historical research meets boots-on-the-ground exploration.

What I Do in the Field

  • Site Surveys: On-location assessment of historical sites, ruins, and potential artifact locations
  • Estate Investigations: Cataloging and evaluating collections in private homes, attics, and storage facilities
  • Archive Deep Dives: In-person research at local historical societies, libraries, and municipal records
  • Recovery Operations: Coordinating ethical recovery of artifacts from agreed-upon locations
  • Documentation: Photography, mapping, and detailed note-taking for provenance records
  • Local Interviews: Speaking with community members, historians, and descendants who hold oral histories

My Fieldwork Approach

Every investigation is different, but my method remains consistent:

  • Pre-Field Research: Thorough preparation using maps, historical documents, and preliminary contacts
  • Local Partnerships: Working with local authorities, landowners, and cultural heritage professionals
  • Systematic Documentation: Detailed records, photographs, and GPS coordinates for every finding
  • Respect for Context: Preserving archaeological context and cultural sensitivity
  • Safety First: Proper equipment, permits, and risk assessment for all field operations

Types of Investigations

My fieldwork has taken me to:

  • Remote archaeological sites requiring multi-day expeditions
  • European manor houses with centuries of accumulated history
  • Abandoned military installations and historical battlefields
  • Private collections in need of assessment and cataloging
  • Archives in small towns where records haven't been digitized
  • Coastal regions for maritime artifact recovery

Equipment & Expertise

Field investigations require specialized tools and knowledge:

  • Metal detection and ground-penetrating radar (when appropriate)
  • Photography and photogrammetry equipment
  • GPS mapping and documentation tools
  • Basic conservation supplies for fragile discoveries
  • Knowledge of local regulations and permit requirements

When You Need Field Investigation

  • You've inherited a property with potential historical significance
  • Historical records suggest artifacts in a specific location
  • You need on-site evaluation of a collection or estate
  • A provenance gap requires physical investigation
  • You're planning an ethical recovery operation
  • Local archives need to be searched in person
